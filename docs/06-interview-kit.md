# Customer Discovery Kit — Hiring/Recruiting Deepfake Fraud Wedge

> For validating the wedge **before building**. Goal: find out whether the pain is real and whether people will *pay* — not to pitch. Built on "The Mom Test" principles: ask about their past and present behavior, never about hypothetical futures.

**Last updated:** 2026-06-19

---

## The #1 Rule

**Do not pitch. Do not describe your idea until the very end (if at all).** The moment you say "I'm building a tool that…", people get polite and lie to be nice. Instead, ask about their *life, past actions, and what they already spend money on*. Facts about the past are reliable; opinions about the future are worthless.

Bad question: *"Would you buy a deepfake detection tool for hiring?"* → everyone says "sure, sounds useful" → meaningless.
Good question: *"Walk me through the last time you suspected a candidate wasn't who they claimed. What did you do?"* → real behavior.

## Who to Talk To (pick from these roles)

| Role | Why they feel the pain |
|---|---|
| **Talent Acquisition / Recruiting leads** | Own the interview pipeline; get burned when a fake candidate slips through. |
| **Heads of HR / People Ops (SMB & mid-market)** | Own hiring outcomes and budget. |
| **IT / Security at companies hiring remote engineers** | North Korean IT worker scheme, insider-threat angle. |
| **Trust & Safety / Fraud ops** (marketplaces, staffing platforms) | Identity fraud at scale. |
| **Staffing agency / RPO owners** | Reputation dies if they place a fraudulent hire. |
| **Background-check / verification resellers** | Might integrate you. |

**Where to find them:** LinkedIn (search titles + DM), r/recruiting, r/talentacquisition, r/cybersecurity, HR/recruiting Slack & Discord communities, talent-acquisition conferences, your own 1st/2nd-degree network.

## Cold Outreach Templates (DM / Email)

**LinkedIn DM (short, no pitch):**
> Hi [Name] — I'm researching how remote-hiring teams handle candidate identity fraud (deepfakes, impersonation, etc.). Not selling anything — just trying to understand the problem from people who actually run interviews. Could I ask you 4–5 quick questions over DM or a 15-min call? Either works.

**Reddit / community post (problem-first, no link):**
> Recruiters/TA folks: have you ever caught — or *missed* — a candidate using a deepfake or impersonating someone in a remote interview? Trying to understand how common this actually is and what teams do about it. War stories welcome.

**Follow-up if they engage:**
> Appreciate it. Two quick ones: (1) when it happened, what did it cost you — time, a bad hire, anything? (2) Did you change your process afterward or buy any tool? Curious what's actually out there that works.

## The Interview Script (15-min call or async DM)

### Warm-up (set the tone — you're a researcher, not a seller)
1. "Thanks for the time. I'm just trying to understand the problem — I'm not here to sell you anything. Mind if I ask how your remote interview/hiring process works today, start to finish?"

### Problem discovery (their reality, past behavior)
2. "How many of your interviews are fully remote / video these days?"
3. "Have you ever had a candidate you suspected wasn't who they claimed — wrong person on camera, AI-assisted answers, identity mismatch? **Walk me through the most recent time.**"
4. "What tipped you off? What did you actually do about it?"
5. "Did one ever get through — someone hired who turned out to be fake or fronted by someone else? What happened?"
6. "When that happens, what does it cost you — wasted interview hours, a bad hire's salary, security risk, your reputation with the hiring manager?"

### Current solutions & spend (the money signal)
7. "What do you do today to verify a candidate is real — ID checks, background checks, anything?"
8. "Who do you pay for that, and roughly what does it cost you per hire or per year?" *(background-check spend is the budget you'd tap)*
9. "Have you ever looked for a tool specifically for deepfake/interview fraud? What did you find? Why did or didn't you buy?"
10. "Whose budget would this come out of — yours, security's, HR's?"

### Severity & urgency (is it a top-3 problem or a someday-problem?)
11. "On a scale where 10 = 'this keeps me up at night,' where does candidate identity fraud rank against your other hiring headaches right now?"
12. "Is this getting worse, better, or about the same over the last year?"

### Soft close (only now hint at solution — to test reaction, not to pitch)
13. "If there were a simple way to flag a likely fake candidate *during* an interview and hand you a report you could act on — would that change anything for you? What would it need to do to be worth paying for?"
14. "What would make you *not* trust or *not* buy something like that?" *(surfaces objections: false positives, bias/legal, candidate friction)*

### The ask (advance the relationship)
15. "This was incredibly helpful. Two things: (a) Could I show you a rough version in a few weeks and get your reaction? (b) Who else dealing with this should I talk to?"

## What You're Listening For (scoring each conversation)

**Green flags (pain is real + buyable):**
- They can name a *specific* incident without prompting.
- They already spend money on background/identity checks (budget exists).
- They tried to find a solution and couldn't, or hacked something together.
- They ask *you* "so when can I try it?" — pull, not push.
- They offer to intro you to others.

**Red flags (move on or change wedge):**
- "Interesting problem, but it's never really happened to us."
- Lots of enthusiasm, zero past action, no budget owner.
- They rank it 2/10 vs other problems.
- Only *you* are excited; they're being polite.

**Decision gate:** After ~12–15 conversations, you want **3–4 people who (a) named a real incident, (b) already have verification budget, and (c) said "show me when it's ready."** If you have that → build the thin MVP. If not → switch wedge (marketplace trust, SMB KYC, voice/contact-center) or rethink.
